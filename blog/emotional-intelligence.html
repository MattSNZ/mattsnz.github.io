Emotional Intelligence

What is EQ?

Emotional intelligence (EQ) is the term used to the group of abilities used to recognise, control, and assess emotions. While some people naturally have high levels of EQ, the good news is that it is also something that can be learned.

In some ways, we can compare EQ with IQ in that they are both ways of measuring the elements of our brains' aptitude. IQ is definitely more well-known and is, problematically, often referred to as the be-all of intelligence measurement. One of the main issues with IQ in this respect is that almost all tests were developed with a very Eurocentric view of the world and ignore large cultural differences that can cause those from other cultures to perform poorly. IQ also fails in that it doesn't take into account how a person interacts with the people around them. This is where EQ shines.

EQ at Work

The modern workplace is a highly collaborative place. Whether within your team, across internal teams, other companies, or with customers, you spend a lot of time dealing with people. Research related to this in the tech industry has shown that there are six factors that tend to separate the top performers from the rest. Of these six, four are closely associated with EQ and only two relate to IQ:

  • Achievement Drive and Standards;
  • Influence;
  • Conceptual Thinking;
  • Initiative;
  • Analytical Ability; and
  • Self-Confidence

With this research in mind it's no surprise that high performing workplaces prefer employees with high levels of EQ, to go alongside their technical abilities. Despite some highly questionable other HR practices, Netflix has long maintained that they harbour no interest in "brilliant jerks" for their teams. This quote is taken directly from the Culture page of their hiring site:

On a dream team, there are no “brilliant jerks.” The cost to teamwork is just too high. Our view is that brilliant people are also capable of decent human interactions, and we insist upon that. When highly capable people work together in a collaborative context, they inspire each other to be more creative, more productive and ultimately more successful as a team than they could be as a collection of individuals.

Similarly, Atlassian has an established policy not to hire these "brilliant jerks" and their performance reviews are structured so that these employees will not be able to artifically shine across all areas of the review.

Other than the benefits of learning about neuroplasticity, the thing that has surprised me the most has been the benefits of pseudocode. Now I just need to remind myself to use it more frequently!

What were the most challenging aspects of the core learning?

At times, the core learning has felt like a distraction from the tech learning. Reminding myself that I'm not hear to just learn the technology but actually how to be a great developer helps me move through that.

Why do you think we, a programming school, are spending so much time focusing on core learning in a web development bootcamp course?

Development is a constantly shifting field. What was common 10, or even 5, years ago is now passé so learning how to implement the new shiny framework or updated standards is incredibly important.

Does the time you spent studying core learning here feel like a waste of time? Should you have just used that time to practising programming instead? Justify your answer.

I don't think so. Some of it was rehashing previously learned things for me, but it was good to go over them again in the context of development. I think my previous answer about the shifting sands of web development justifies this feeling quite well.

Can I improve?

The good news is that your brain is amazing. It has the power to learn anything you want. Three things to focus on if you want to improve your EQ are:

  • Listen:

    Take the time to listen to what people are trying to tell you, both verbally and non-verbally.

  • Empathise:

    Picking up on emotions is critical, but you also need to be able to put yourself into someone else's shoes in order to truly understand their point of view.

  • Question:

    Consider how your own emotions influence your decisions and behaviors.

    Why is this person feeling this way? Are there any unseen factors that might be contributing to these feelings? How to your emotions differ from theirs?
